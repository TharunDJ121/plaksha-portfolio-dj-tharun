Chapter One
Curiosity started it.

Where it all
began.

2020. Lockdown. A teenager with too much time, a laptop, and a question that wouldn't go away: How do things actually work?

01

The Question That Started Everything

Stuck at home during lockdown, I started wondering how the games I played were actually made. That single question cracked something open. I found HTML, then CSS, then JavaScript — and didn't stop.

02

Too Far, Too Fast

I jumped into Unity before I understood the fundamentals. It crashed, nothing made sense, and I nearly gave up. That frustration taught me something — patience is part of the process, not the enemy of it.

03

Discovering Arduino

Someone mentioned Arduino. Robotics. Physical computing. The idea that you could write code and make something in the real world move — I was hooked. I borrowed books. I watched tutorials at midnight.

04

First Success — With My Father

My first Bluetooth-controlled robotic car worked. My father watched it move across the floor. He didn't say much, but the look on his face said everything. That was the moment I knew I'd found something real.

🧵

The Lesson

Curiosity alone is energy without direction. Discipline is what turns a question into a creation. I learned to sit with confusion, to fail quietly, and to keep going anyway. That habit became the foundation for everything that followed.

Chapter Three
Failure sharpened me.

The moment that
changed everything.

Big Bang. A national-stage competition. Our best work — and our biggest lesson in what actually makes a team work.

The Setup

We built something we were proud of.

Our architecture was solid. The technical execution was strong. We believed in the project. We went into the competition with confidence.

What Went Wrong

Communication broke down under pressure.

The team wasn't on the same page during the evaluation window. Roles blurred. Critical moments were missed not because of what we built, but because of how we'd failed to communicate when it mattered most.

The Turning Point

I sat with that feeling. And then I changed.

I used to think leading a team meant pointing the right direction. I learned it means making sure everyone can see the path — before the pressure arrives, not during it.

What I Carried Forward

A completely different approach to collaboration.

Structure. Checklists. Clear ownership. But more than that — the habit of checking in on people, not just on tasks. The best architecture in the world fails if the team isn't connected to each other.

Before
  • Assumed everyone was aligned
  • Focused on what to build, not how to work
  • Communication was reactive
  • Leading from the front, not alongside
After
  • Explicit check-ins before every milestone
  • Shared documentation and role clarity
  • Proactive communication under pressure
  • Leadership as shared accountability

Chapter Five
Impact gave it meaning.

Leadership as
empathy.

Some of the things I'm most proud of weren't on any project board. They were moments where showing up — with courage or care — was the whole point.

🌈

Solo Performance for the Transgender Community

I performed alone on stage, representing the transgender community — a group often invisible in my school's environment. It was uncomfortable. It was necessary. Courage isn't the absence of fear; it's choosing visibility when silence would be easier.

🚨

Youth Addiction Awareness

Collaborated with local police on substance abuse awareness programmes for young people. Brought school and community together around a problem that needed honest voices.

💬

A Friend Who Needed Help

Noticed a friend struggling and helped guide them toward professional counseling. Sometimes the most impactful thing you can do is stay present long enough to see what others overlook.

These weren't grand gestures. They were decisions to act when it would have been easier not to. That pattern — noticing, caring, doing — is what I want to carry into every space I enter.

Why Interdisciplinary Matters to Me

The problems I care about — urban sustainability, civic access, environmental monitoring — don't sit inside one discipline. They need someone who can move between hardware, software, policy, and people. That's the kind of thinker I'm deliberately becoming.

Technology That Solves Real Problems

Not demos. Not prototypes for portfolios. I want to build things that get deployed, that handle edge cases, that people actually use — and that work better because I thought carefully about the human on the other end.

What I'm Looking For Next

An environment where curiosity is a credential. Where I can learn rigorously, build meaningfully, and work alongside people who take both technology and responsibility seriously.

The Thread That Runs Through Everything

From that first Arduino project with my father to leading a school of a thousand students — the common thread is this: I see a problem, I ask how it works, and I don't stop until I've learned something real.

"I don't want to be remembered for what I built. I want to be remembered for what those things made possible — for the people who used them, the communities they reached, and the students who saw it and thought: I could do that too."
